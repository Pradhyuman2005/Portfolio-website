Case Study- Eco Living Planner App

Research Prototypes A/B Test Final Mockup Impact

Role- UI/UX Designer & Researcher

Project Duration- 6 weeks

Description- Users struggle to adopt sustainable habits due to lack of simple, time-efficient guidance.

View Figma Prototype

Problem Statement

Urban users struggle to incorporate sustainable habits into their daily routines due to time constraints, lack of guidance, and absence of tools that seamlessly integrate eco-friendly actions into everyday planning.

Research

Investigating existing solutions

Key Takeaways

1. The UI of this app is really easy to use, I should also plan on making something similar.
2. The app currently targets a niche audience of sustainability enthusiasts; to drive real
impact, it must appeal to a broader, less-engaged audience as well.

Key Takeaways

1. This app is not very user centric and may confound the users in some aspects.
Hence, I will place user convenience to the highest.
2. I will respect users’ time and energy by not wasting it.

Key Takeaways

1. Scanning items idea seems pretty interesting, maybe we can use it some other way.
2. I liked the thorough transparency of this app’s suggestions,alternatives etc. It helps
build trust, we can integrate this.
3. Suggesting alternatives to the user is a better idea rather than shaming them.

Key Takeaways

1. Gamification is a good technique to improve user retention.
2. Incorporating social features enhances engagement, as users are more inclined toward shared, group-based
experiences than solo interactions.
3. Avoid oversimplification; the app should include depth, such as explaining the science behind
sustainable practices, to nurture user curiosity.

User Groups

Budget concious students

1. Age- 18-24
2. Tech-savvy
3. Goal- Aggressively save money, Use smart hacks to budget their expenses.
4. Likely to shop offline in market. More open to experimentation.
5. Might care less about sustainability but more about saving money.

Busy working parents

1. Age- 25-45
2. Medium tech comfort
3. Goal- Save time, avoid distress and frustration, buy in bulk, reduce household
waste
4. They may care about sustainability, but prioritize saving time, preferring quick, efficient solutions over experimentation.

Eco-motivated professionals

1. Age- 25-40
2. Highly aware of the importance of sustainability and are likely tech-savvy
3. Likely to spend a few extra minutes in learning creative ways to practice sustainability
4. Goal- Incorporate sustainability and discover new ways to become sustainable. Open to experimentation.
5. Preference- low-effort, high-impact tools
6. Trigger: guilt, their impact on overall sustainability realm in the world

Personas

Final summary

How my decisions will influence my design


1. Users value sustainability but act on it only when it fits their time and budget.
Design influence: The app prioritises quick, practical value through features like item scanning, health analysis,
and cheaper sustainable alternatives. The dashboard highlights metrics such as money saved and
environmental impact, reinforcing existing user motivation.



2. Users hesitate to use the app if it feels untrustworthy, complex, or overloaded with text.
Design influence: The app will be minimal and intuitive, with visual, concise information. It will prioritise trust
and authenticity, using limited location tracking (e.g., geofencing) to respect privacy. The
tone will remain calm and reliable, and complex features like AR are removed in favour of
simple, manual interactions such as checking item details and tracking impact.



3. Users rely on social proof and are more likely to adopt the app through trusted recommendations.
Design influence: The app will incorporate elements like testimonials and a community-driven tone to reinforce
trust and reliability.

A/B Testing



Difficulties and challenges by users in testing

User 1

● Sustainability metrics seemed inaccurate.
● The top bar created false affordance.
● The image icon looked clickable (false affordance).
● The orange barcode button was preferred.
● Screen B had poor contrast.
● Profile details felt unnecessary.
● The “2/10” option lacked clear affordance.



User 2

● Pie graph animations should be improved.
● The top line creates false affordance (looks swipeable).
● Screen B feels more friendly, with a clearer, larger pie graph.
● Personal profile (name and photo) was appreciated.
● The ‘save’ button was hard to tap, indicating padding issues.
● Screen B’s ‘factors’ feature felt better due to icons and animations.



User 3

● Navigation felt easy due to familiarity with similar apps.
● Highlighting the home icon is clearer than using the top bar.
● The photo icon created confusion (looked clickable).
● Screen B was preferred for its clean and aesthetic design.
● Personal profile feature was liked.
● Accessibility options were hard to tap, indicating padding issues.
● Wanted clear indicators for product health.
● Product comparison feature was appreciated.



User 4

● The pie graph should be interactive.
● The top bar created false affordance (looked swipeable).
● Metrics felt smaller due to the enlarged pie graph.
● Screen B was preferred for its familiar and welcoming design.
● Contrasting tick boxes in B felt counterintuitive; A was preferred.
● The experience was often compared to other familiar apps.
● No major issues were found in the barcode scanner.



User 5

● The UI felt easy to understand and navigate.
● No-login in version A was appreciated.
● Suggested combining strengths of both versions; preferred A for interactivity.
● Showed low interest in the profile page.
● Focus leaned toward saving money.
● Product comparison feature was liked.
